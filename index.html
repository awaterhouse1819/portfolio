Flagship · Esquire Bank (NASDAQ: ESQ) · 2025

The Intent Bet: finding a behavioral signal hiding inside a compliance-built audience.

Tested an unused ZoomInfo intent signal against geographic targeting in a 4K-vs-4K controlled split inside SFMC. Intent cohort beat control on every KPI. Layered model shipped to production.

4K×2 Controlled cohorts · intent vs geography
Lift Across every KPI in the test
Shipped Layered model in production next quarter

Problem

Esquire's audience is narrow on purpose (plaintiffs' law firms) and segmented by geography because IOLTA rules are state-specific. The structure made sense for compliance. The side effect was that every firm inside a given state looked identical on the way into a campaign.

I didn't want to go shopping for a new tool. Before asking for budget, I wanted to know what we already had. What's flowing into the CRM today, and where is it coming from? I walked every source into Salesforce: form fills, BDO activity, enrichment vendors, a ZoomInfo intent feed that was landing in the CRM but wasn't being used for anything. The signal was already there. Nobody had instrumented it.

Approach

If intent predicts revenue-qualified engagement better than geography, the change isn't a campaign. It's the audience model. Geography becomes the compliance layer. Intent becomes the targeting layer. We stop choosing between them.

  • Mapped every source feeding the CRM and marked which fields were actually being used. The ZoomInfo intent signal came out of that audit.
  • Designed a clean split test: 4,000 intent-flagged firms against 4,000 matched controls. Same creative, same cadence, same sender. IOLTA rules held constant on both sides.
  • Walked the intent signal through legal and compliance before it touched a send, routing it through our FDIC-compliant preference model so the test could actually ship.
  • Instrumented it at the data-extension layer in SFMC so we could tie every KPI (opens, clicks, BDO meetings, downstream pipeline) back to the cohort without guesswork.

Result

The intent cohort beat the control on every KPI. We shipped the layered model the next quarter, with geography still carrying compliance and intent carrying targeting, and the team kept the test framework for the next hypothesis. The part I keep coming back to: the best signal we had wasn't a new purchase. It was sitting in the CRM, waiting for someone to ask what it was doing there.

02  /  Method

How I think about an email program.

Four layers I work through, in order. None of them is new on its own. The compounding happens when all four are actually instrumented together, which, in most programs I've walked into, they aren't.

  1. 01

    Who we're actually sending to

    Segmentation built on behavior and intent, not demographic proxies. If a segment can't be tested, suppressed, or tied to a revenue motion, it's decoration.

    • Intent signals
    • Behavioral cohorts
    • Suppression logic
  2. 02

    Creative as infrastructure

    A template library, content blocks, and a QA protocol that let the team ship faster without drifting on brand or compliance. Stop rebuilding. Start reusing.

    • Template library
    • AMPscript blocks
    • Litmus QA
  3. 03

    Tests that compound

    Every test gets a written hypothesis, a pre-declared success metric, and a row in the log. Over a year, the log becomes a library of what actually works in this audience.

    • Hypothesis-first
    • Pre-declared metrics
    • Test log
  4. 04

    Reporting that closes the loop

    Dashboards that tie sends to revenue, not open rates in isolation. Weekly, monthly, quarterly rhythms. And a recommendation at the end of every readout. Otherwise it's just a screenshot.

    • KPI per stream
    • Revenue attribution
    • Sales feedback loop

04  /  Perspective

What I believe about email in 2026.

Short positions. Each one is something I've tested in a live, regulated program, not a thought-leadership post.

i.

Open rates are a diagnostic, not a goal.

Apple MPP made opens a noisy signal. They're still the fastest read on list health and subject-line craft, but the moment a team starts targeting them, the number stops meaning anything. Read it daily, report it quarterly, don't optimize for it.

ii.

Segmentation is the ceiling.

Most programs spend on creative and under-invest in who they're sending to. The intent test at Esquire made it embarrassing how clear this is. Same creative, same cadence, better audience. Lift on every KPI. The signal worth betting on usually isn't new. It's already in the CRM, nobody's used it yet.

iii.

Regulation is a design constraint, not a blocker.

Working inside FDIC, FINRA, and privacy rules forces cleaner systems: better consent, tighter suppression, a real audit trail. I've stopped treating compliance as the thing slowing me down. It's usually the thing that made the program durable enough to scale.

iv.

The template library is strategy.

A reusable, accessibility-tested, compliance-safe template system is boring. It's also the highest-leverage thing an email leader can actually ship. Every send after it goes faster, and fewer of them break.

v.

AI belongs in the QA layer first.

Before AI writes the subject line, let it pressure-test the one you wrote. Before it generates creative, let it audit your dark-mode rendering. The boring uses (QA, accessibility, deliverability) pay off faster than the ones people are posting about.

vi.

Sales feedback is the missing KPI.

Opens and clicks tell you if the email worked. The BDO team tells you if the lead was real. Closing that loop, and actually reporting on it, is what turns email from a channel into a revenue function.
